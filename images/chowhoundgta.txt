What's the different between cured bacon vs. #uncured #bacon.  The curing process can be different depending on where you live. There will be salt involved in the curing and depending on regulations where you live, often some source of nitrate or nitrite, to prevent unwanted bacteria. A pleasant side effect of these additions is the deepening of the red/pink color many of us associate with bacon, though some of the redness of the meat is based on the diet of the animal too. Uncured bacon is not from nitrates or nitrites, but instead cured with (most commonly) celery juice. Usually saltpeter, or curing salt is added to the celery juice to get the same effects of bacteria resistant meat.  Not much of any of these ingredients is needed. What makes the cures taste different (besides the saltiness) is the other kinds of herbs, spices, and sweeteners used in the cure mixture. Texture wise, the main difference between cured and uncured bacon is that the uncured bacon will have a much higher water content than the cured bacon, so the fat will not be as dense. #breakfast #picoftheday #food #uncuredbacon #baconlovers #simplepleasures #paleo #fathersday #foodfacts #foodmatters

#Roti is eaten widely across the West Indies, especially in countries with large Indo-Caribbean populations such as #Trinidad and #Tobago.  As Caribbean's moved to North American cities such as Toronto, New York, and Montreal, they exported with them the wrapped version of roti. This iconic version in the photo is called #Dhalpuri.  It is a roti with a stuffing of ground yellow split peas, #cumin (#geera), garlic, and pepper. The split peas are boiled until they are al dente and then ground in a mill. The cumin is toasted until black and also ground. The stuffing is pushed into the roti dough, and sealed. When rolled flat, the filling is distributed within the roti. It is cooked on the #tava and rubbed with oil for ease of #cooking. This type of roti is most commonly eaten with a variety of curries. This is a curried goat roti. #Doubles (bottom left pic) is a common street food in Trinidad and Tobago. It is a sandwich made with two bara (flat fried bread) filled with #channa (curried chick peas). It can be topped with mango, shadon beni, cucumber, coconut, tamarind, and always with extra pepper sauce.  This delicacy is the most popular fast food in Trinidad and Tobago.
