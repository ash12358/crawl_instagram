As it is the celebration of the end of chattel Slavery, we might expect it to be a major American holiday like Labor Day or Memorial Day...but somehow it's not. . The more complicated answer is that institutionally most states willfully omit Black history within education. Teaching Tolerance, a project of the Southern Poverty Law Center, in 2014 graded all 50 states and the District of Columbia on how well their public schools taught the civil-rights era to students. Twenty states received a failing grade, and in five states—Alaska, Iowa, Maine, Oregon, and Wyoming—civil-rights education was totally absent from state standards. . Bottom line: Black history (arguably one of America's most important histories) is willfully undervalued, and as a result, the importance of a day like today is ignored. . Your PSA of the Day > To the Black community, #MilesDavis was a symbol of the uncompromising Black artist, named by Life magazine as one of the people most influential in advancing the prestige of #BlackAmerica. . He refused to talk during performances, instead standing silently, sometimes even turning his back on his increasingly white audiences. He may have enjoyed baiting them, defying the code that said #jazzmusicians —especially #Blackjazzmusicians —were required to hoke things up. Miles Davis attacked this notion on all fronts...just look at #interview he gave in 1962: . “About the first thing I can remember as a little boy was a white man running me down a street hollering ‘Nigger! Nigger!’ My father went hunting him with a shotgun. Being sensitive and having race pride has been in my family since slave days. The slave Davises played classical string music on the plantations. My father, Miles the first, was born six years after the Emancipation. He wanted to play music, but my grandfather wanted him to be an entertainer for white folks.” . “See white people have certain things they expect from Negro musicians. It goes clear back to the slavery days. That was when Uncle Tomming got started because white people demanded it...Every little Black child grew up seeing that getting along with white people meant grinning and acting clowns. It helped white people to feel easy about what they had done, and were doing, to Negroes, and that's carried right on over to now. You bring it down to musicians, they want you to not only play your instrument, but to entertain them, too, with grinning and dancing.” . “But I ain't no model, and I don't sing or dance, and I damn sure ain't no Uncle Tom just to be up there grinning. When I’m working, I’m concentrating. I bet you if I was a doctor sewing on some son of a bitch’s heart, they wouldn’t want me to talk.” . Miles Davis operated during a time when Black musicians were often expected to demean themselves for the purposes of entertainment. Today is the day that our nation takes pause to celebrate the service and sacrifice of our military heroes that gave their lives to secure our freedoms. The earliest commemoration of what would later become Memorial Day, was organized in a former Confederate Prison Camp in #CharlestonSouthCarolina on May 1, 1865. The celebration was established by over 1,000 newly freed African Americans, in addition to U.S. Colored Troops regiments and a small group of white Charlestonians. This group came together to honor the 257 Union soldiers that died as a result of the poor conditions of the Confederate Prison Camp during the war. They removed the soldiers from a mass grave that the #Confederates made and created proper burial grounds for #Unionsoldiers. Together they sang hymns, set flowers, and gave readings in honor of the soldiers’ sacrifice. #MemorialDay #CharlestonSC #theholycity  #MilitaryAppreciationMonth #APeoplesJourney #ANationsStory #blackhistory #africanamericanhistory 📸: Provost Guard of the 107th Colored Infantry, Fort Corcoran, Washington, D.C., 1863

FACT OF THE DAY from @uncolonial_history: There’s been thousands of books and movies made about #Naziconcentrationcamps such as Auschwitz and Buchenwald, but hardly anything written about their true origins. In one of the first genocides of the 1900’s, German settler colonists invented the modern day concentration camp, and this idea would then be used again only a few decades later in the Holocaust. So where was the German concentration camp really born? The answer might surprise you... . In 1884, Germany declared a “protectorate” over Namibia (at the time the colony was called Southwest Africa). More and more Germans moved into the colony, but there was limited space for them. Meanwhile the Herero were a nation of people that held the largest amount of land. Knowing this, it shouldn’t be surprising that long before an “unfortunate war” broke out, early German governors of the colony stated that “in the long run...we anticipate having to destroy the existing Indigenous social and political systems to facilitate economic development.” . Eventually a war did break out, but it was largely a war that the colonists forced into existence. After the main Herero force was defeated, the German army under Von Trotha chased thousands of non combatants into the desert. Von Trotha ordered a famous “extermination order” and starved the fleeing non-combatants. . The surviving Herero and Nama people were put into one of the world’s first modern concentration camps. One camp was especially brutal: Shark Island. During the period of October 1906 to March 1907, 1,032 died out of a total of 1,795. The Herero were subjected to unchecked rape, beatings, and executions. The Herero and the Nama were forced into labor and literally worked to death. . Today, #German tourists still flock to Namibia on holidays. It’s really common for tourists to rent ATV’s and ride them through the desert...the same desert that Herero men, women, and children were hunted down. And #SharkIsland, the concentration camp? Mary Eliza Mahoney, America's first black trained #nurse, was born #OTD in 1845. Known as the first professional #AfricanAmerican nurse, #MaryElizaMahoney became a #registerednurse after graduating from the New England Hospital for Women and Children’s nursing school in 1879.
