I recently received this lens from a nice lady whose grandfather was a successful photographer in the New York area and used this very glass in his portraiture studio at the beginning of last century. The lens is a Bausch and Lomb 500mm f4.5 and it is not in pristine conditions ( the original owner dates it circa 1918) but it is still a great performer. It's mounted on a 9x9 inch lens board coupled to the largest Packard shutter I have ever come across, with an large opening of over 5 inches in diameter. Not owning a studio camera accepting such a large lens board (such an ANSCO or similar) and curious to test the lens, I crudely strapped the beastly optic to a tripod strong enough to sustain the 15 pounds it weights, and then built out of black velveteen, a rudimentary bag bellows to attach the contraption to the front standard of my 8x10 Deardorff camera. This is the fist test shot I made on paper negative. Extremely happy with the portraiture potential of this lens, I shall keep experimenting with it, while actively looking around for a camera accepting a 9x9 lens boards and retaining at least some degree of portability. #largeformat #heavylens #fifteenpoundslens #portraitlens #portraiture #oldlens

SHOOTING DRY GLASS PLATES In the rapidly shrinking landscape of film stocks and, by now, the lonely desert of instant film availability, I thought it was time to experiment with oblique strategies (as always preached by Brian Eno). So I was most interested to discover another pioneer of a much needed anti-technocracy renaissance who is producing glass plates. It is only the beginning of production and there are still flaws in the coating of the plates as you can see from my image, but that is surely bound to be solved in future batches. I am very excited by the results. I only tested half a dozen plates, so far, but the results are very encouraging, and certainly worth further experimenting. This is indeed a viable alternative for me to the usual orthochromatic film I use for my portraiture. The modus operandi is very straight forward, something that was never so with the various attempts to re-produce polaroid-like instant films, after the demise of Polaroid. When everything else fails: EMBRACE YOUR MISTAKES ! I took a 4x5 Graflex for a stroll downtown today, wide angle lens, hand held. This was after months of not shooting anything in the street, by choice and necessity (I am still limping badly). So, I did - everything I could do - wrong. Out of a Graflex magazine of 6 negatives I managed to fog three, the other three, I double exposed twice or three times ( I lost count). Back in my darkroom, I threw the three surviving negatives, all together, in an overly hot developer soup, left them there and went to make myself a coffee.  An unspecified amount of time later, I pulled them from the developer, I under-fixed them, under-washed them and just chucked them on the kitchen window to dry. This is my favorite one of the lot.

I assume all Americans know the meaning of a raised middle finger, and so do most citizens of the world, by now. The real question is who or what do we raise our middle finger to. But I found no clear answers to my question. It felt as if that finger was mainly raised towards ourselves, towards our fellow Americans, towards all those frustrations, anger, injustices, we appear to have now decided to hate and despise, to angrily, violently fight against, not to debate anymore with decency, in search of solutions and civil compromises. Straight out of my coffee- flavored darkroom,one of the first developed images, a lovely shot, in my opinion, from the latest portraiture sitting with model and actress extraordinaire @miszjez !! A real pleasure working with her ! Shot on 4x5 paper negative (3ASA), Tachihara camera, Fuji 250mm Soft Focus lens, developed in coffee. No Photoshop or retouching whatsoever was applied. Instead I achieved the soft look by using a portraiture lens (soft focus), an extremely low ASA rating (3), and a very careful illumination with roughly 2400 Watts per second strobe heads. The harsher is the  light on the subject, the softer the glow on the highlights, with this type of lens.
