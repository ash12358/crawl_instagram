Tw: talk about hijab and antiblackness. For Muslim femmes or their sisters who have #kinkyhair/#naturalhair/#curlyhair/ wore or wear #hijab. #personalthoughts Theres a lot of celebration for hijab and thats cool. I wore hijab for 15 years of my life, it was something I fought for, got harassed for, eventually got islamoracism pstd.. It was my middle finger to society and kept my fighting spirit alive, etc. But I also want to open the conversation.  Some aspects of my hijab wearing was helpful and what I really needed in my life-- boundaries, healing, solidarity etc-- but looking back I think some of the cultures around my hijab wearing was rooted in antiblackness. I was constantly told that my hair needed to be fixed, that I looked better covered, that hijabis with my hair texture have been divorced on their wedding nights upon taking off their hijabs... And always I noticed surprisingly a large amount of hijabis had natural hair. Although my mixed mother never inherited her fathers hair and passed as white and didn't know how take care of my hair she was the one person in my life that always told me that my hair was beautiful in an environment that told me I needed to be fixed. After taking off my hijab (and embarking on a journey of haircare), many bad blowouts, diy relaxers, 1000s of $ of wasted products, loving friends support and YouTube tutorials later I've finally in my mid 20s learned more or less how to take care of my hair and have started to believe that my hair is beautiful (not only when blown out but also curly). I want to warn my hijabi communities and build awareness that hijab can and has been used as a vehicle for shame, control, antiblackness and classism (in the past there were laws that enslaved women were not allowed to wear hijab and it was a trend of the elite) --and to combat that aspect and encourage culture of hijab wearing that promotes bodily autonomy and alternative beauty standards. Your thoughts and contributions are appreciated to better hash this out.

Homage to the unibrow, facial hair and Iran's Qajar-era gender bending beauty standards. "The Qajar era (1796-1925) beauty standard sure was interesting. There is, however, an explanation for this. In Iran, the concept of beauty was not viewed as intrinsically feminine, in fact, it was not gendered in Iranian society and both male and female could perpetuate aesthetic and desirable ‘beauty’. However, Qajar society valued masculinity and the concept of beauty, amongst elite and upper class Iranians, was then masculinized—women, most notably in the Shah’s harem, aimed to render their features more masculine by joining their eyebrows together and growing facial hair. It was only until the the 20th century that Iran, taking from Western ideologies and concepts of sexuality and gender [as well as the intrusion of the Farangi, ie the European/Western gaze, upon the intimate world of Iranian homosociality between both men and women, and the Western gaze perceived this homosociality as something to be condemned and as a symbol of “backwardness”], that the idea of beauty became solely attributed to femininity, to direct the Iranian male gaze towards the sexualized female, and heterosexuality was normalized as Iranian modernity and patriotism, whereas before the 20th century, bi-romanticism and bisexuality were not uncommon [in fact, the daughter of Nasser al-Din Shah Qajar, Taj Al-Saltaneh, wrote in her memoirs about an affair she had with a young seamstress she knew. Before her, Sufi poetry also used implied or explicit reference to love and affection between men, normally of age difference, as a spiritual aspect of Sufism and recognition of the Divine]. The beauty standard also shifted to emulate the aesthetic of the European woman as the conventional image of what is desirable. The Qajar era was also a grave period of time for Iran, as the hallmarks of the time were political and civil unrest, extreme financial despair, and the notion that the Shah had sold out Iran to Western powers." --ask-Iran #ilanasart #iran #queeryourbeauty #beautystandards
